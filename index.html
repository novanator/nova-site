Resume/CV

Nicole Nova, MSc, DDS


I'm a Research Associate at the Department of Biology at Duke University. I work on mathematical modeling in viral evolutionary dynamics in the Koelle Research Group. I'm modeling the immune system response to HIV infection, in order to provide some insight that might be useful for development of potential HIV vaccines in the future.

Previously, I was in the Michor Lab at the Department of Biostatistics and Computational Biology, Dana-Farber Cancer Institute affiliated with Harvard Medical School and Harvard School of Public Health. I was working on mathematical modeling, especially stochastic processes, and computer simulations of cancer development and progression. The process of the rise of mutations relevant to cancer is an application in evolutionary dynamics, a merge between evolutionary biology and mathematics.

In 2012, I earned my Doctor of Dental Surgery (DDS) degree and a Master of Science degree in Dental Surgery from Karolinska Institute in Sweden. As an Erasmus Mundus scholar I also studied abroad at Queen Mary's School of Medicine and Dentistry in London, UK. Additionally, I did a clinical internship at the Department of Head and Neck Surgery at the Medical University of Vienna, Austria.

I was a research student in the Molecular Pain Lab in the Department of Physiology and Pharmacology at Karolinska Institute. The research I conducted also became a part of my master's thesis in collaboration with the Department of Oral Physiology. It dealt with the pathophysiology of chronic pain in rheumatoid arthritis based on an animal study. This type of academic research is important in order to develop effective pharmaceutics for pain relief. However, it takes approximately 20 years from academic research to a drug on the market!

Today we certainly have the technology to shorten the span from basic research to a final drug product, we just need to know how to incorporate it. For example, clinical trials can be computationally simulated, providing results orders of magnitude faster than using the traditional empirical approach. This enables a reliable and fast screening process and evidently reduces the number of clinical trials needed to confirm the effects in vivo.

During my years of clinical experience and biomedical research, I saw an opportunity for emerging technologies to improve clinical outcomes. In order to build a quantitative foundation, in addition to my medical background, I studied electrical engineering at the Royal Institute of Technology in Sweden. I would contend that there is a lot to be gained from interdisciplinary work; with the help of engineering, computer science and mathematics we can find alternative solutions to clinical problems.

Thus, my current fields of interest are: Systems Biology, Computational Biology, Mathematical Modeling in Biology and Evolutionary Biology.

Surgery - Problems to work on in the future

Water Cooling in Dentistry

Decreases the visibility for dentist's and comfort for patients

Water cooling is needed when drilling or laser cutting on teeth. This is to ensure that the heat from the drill/laser doesn't kill the tooth. The suction tool used in dentistry is the only device for removing the water building up inside the patients mouth. Isn't there another solution to the overheating problem?

Drop the hooks!

Old-fashioned way of stabilizing flaps in surgery

The key thing in surgery is clear vision of the site in question. Peripheral tissues (e.g. skin/muscles) need to be cut and the flaps folded to the side to open up. While the leading surgeon is active (i.e. cutting and removing pathological tissues such as tumors and cysts, etc.) the other surgeons assist with suction (of water/blood) and holding the flaps apart with hooks. I had the experience of holding the hooks for 6 hours straight! The physical exhaustion of my arms and shoulders was burdensome. Especially because you have to be completely still most of the time. Sometimes you'll move the hooks in a manner to favor the work of the active surgeon, which requires the skill of being one step ahead. You also have to constantly weigh the two opposing actions: not taking up a lot of space, and maximizing the opening. So even though holding the hooks seems to be a static activity, it's not that simple to just replace the assisting surgeon with a passive tool with fixed hooks. A machine with some level of intelligence is needed. The machine is crucial here because A) it can be programmed to be completely still, and B) it doesn't experience muscle fatigue and pain.

Imaging - Problems to work on in the future

Ultimate X-Ray Precision During Surgery

Saves time, blood, and iatrogenic injuries

Guided surgery is a relatively new field. There exists technological equipment for guiding the surgeon inside tissue via bleeping sounds. The device can interpret the 3D digital images of tissues from the X-ray images. The device also comes with a sensor that allows the software to compare reality from the 3D image. However, these devices very are expensive and in practice it's hard to say if the bleeping communication is an order of magnitude improvement of the surgeon's abilities to know where to go. Our brain is still far better at visual processing than any computer of today. However, we are unable to keep the whole 3D X-ray image and superimpose it on the actual patient with very high accuracy. That computers can do very well. Why not combine these two facts, and conclude that augmented reality in surgery is the thing to go for.

Counting cells in microscopy

There is a need for a reliable program to count cells in immunohistochemistry

Imagine counting dots on images where the range is hundreds to thousands, and the number of images ranges between 10 - 100. That's a lot of dots to count! Prone for human error! That's why the process is repeated by the same person and also by someone else. Tedious, yes, but also very time-consuming. There is software that can make counting cells under a microscope a little easier. You can take a magnified picture of the slide showing the fluorescent dots from the immunohistochemistry procedure. To know exactly which dots are actual cells, you can take another picture of the same slide but lit with a "control light wavelength". Using an image editing software you can superimpose the two images and see which dots are active cells - the targets for counting. But the actual cell counting procedure is not automated yet (at least that was still the case when I was working on my Master's Thesis). ImageJ provides you with the tool to label each counted cell with a number to keep track. That's good, but the manual counting is something that needs to be improved! There's also the option of having the program counting the dots of a certain color for you, but not all dots are cells, and there is a lot of noise. This makes the results pretty unreliable. My intention is to tackle this problem and find a reliable algorithm.

Robotics

2013

Autonomous Robot Accomplishing Standstill Balance and Forward Motion Using Segway Technology, Royal Institute of Technology (KTH)

Basically I worked in a team of five KTH electrical engineering students to deliver a functioning hardware/software prototype in less than four weeks. The code was written in C although most of the functions came from a library called ROBOTC (courtesy of Carnegie Mellon University) that was compatible with the hardware. We used Lego Mindstorms for the hardware and building components to build a little robot that would stand and balance on two wheels. The next challenge was to make it go forward, which also got accomplished. We also hacked together a compatible file that would take the standard tunes and make it play The Ride of the Valkyries by Wagner (as a geeky fun thing). We named the robot Valkyria and she is a beauty. We later took her to a fair at KTH and gave a presentation, and had her parade on the catwalk. So proud!


The diagram below describes the algorithm used for the robot to maintain its balance on two wheels. It's based on the Proportional Integral Derivative (PID) controller, which is a feedback system for automation and control. The sensor was a gyroscope, and the actuator was motor output to the wheels. Basically what happens is that the gyroscope input is defined as the velocity, and then the acceleration and the position are calculated by taking the derivative and the integral respectively. The sum of all three values is being sent through a filter (another piece of code) to extract values closer to reality, and then sent to the actuators for stabilizing the robot. Done!
